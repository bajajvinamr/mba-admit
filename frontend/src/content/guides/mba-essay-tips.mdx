# MBA Essay Tips: What Admissions Committees Actually Want

Getting your MBA essay right is the difference between an admit and a rejection. We've analyzed 341 real successful essays and 67,000+ admissions decisions. Here's what works.

## The 5 Rules That Matter

### 1. Be Specific, Not Generic

The biggest mistake in MBA essays is writing sentences that could apply to anyone. "I want to make an impact" means nothing. "I want to build a fintech lending platform for India's 300 million unbanked adults" means everything.

**Bad:** "I've always been passionate about leadership."
**Good:** "When our team's project manager quit mid-launch, I volunteered to lead the 14-person team through a 6-week sprint that delivered $2.3M in new revenue."

### 2. Show Failure, Not Just Success

Admissions committees read thousands of "I succeeded" essays. The ones that stand out show vulnerability. The best essays from our 341-essay corpus all include a moment where the applicant failed, was uncertain, or changed their mind.

### 3. Answer the Actual Question

30% of rejected essays in our database fail this basic test. "What matters most to you and why?" is not "Tell us about your career." "Why our school?" is not "Why MBA in general."

### 4. Connect Past → Present → Future

Every strong essay follows this arc:
- **Past:** What shaped you (specific experience, not childhood)
- **Present:** Where you are and what's missing
- **Future:** Why THIS school fills the gap

### 5. Sound Like a Human

Schools cross-check essay voice against interviews and recommendations. If your essay sounds like ChatGPT, that's a red flag. Use contractions. Include sensory details. Write the way you'd explain this to a smart friend over coffee.

## School-Specific Tips

### Harvard Business School
- Open-ended prompt: "What more would you like us to know?"
- No word limit, but 800-1000 words is the sweet spot
- They want to see JUDGMENT, not just achievement

### Stanford GSB
- "What matters most to you, and why?" — the hardest essay in MBA admissions
- Be deeply personal. Stanford wants to understand your values, not your resume
- 650-word limit means every sentence must earn its place

### Wharton
- Two short essays: career goals + personal contribution
- Be very specific about post-MBA plans
- Mention specific Wharton resources (courses, clubs, professors)
