## The GMAT Landscape in 2026

The GMAT remains the most widely accepted standardized test for MBA admissions, though an increasing number of programs now accept the GRE or have gone test-optional. That said, a strong GMAT score still carries significant weight at most top programs — it signals quantitative readiness and provides admissions committees with a standardized benchmark.

Here is what you need to know about GMAT expectations at every tier.

## M7 Schools: The 720+ Club

The seven most selective MBA programs — Harvard Business School, Stanford GSB, Wharton, Booth, Kellogg, Columbia, and MIT Sloan — share a common GMAT profile. Median scores cluster between 730 and 740, with the middle 80% range spanning roughly 700 to 770.

Does this mean you need a 730+ to get in? Not necessarily. A 710 with an exceptional profile — think startup founder, military officer with combat deployments, or professional athlete — can absolutely earn an admit. But if your profile is "standard" (consultant or banker, 3.5 GPA, solid extracurriculars), you will want to be at or above the median.

## T15 Schools: The 700–720 Sweet Spot

Schools ranked 8 through 15 — Tuck, Haas, Ross, Fuqua, Darden, Stern, Yale SOM, and UCLA Anderson — have median GMATs in the 710–725 range. The practical threshold here is 700: scoring below that does not disqualify you, but it does mean the rest of your application needs to do heavier lifting.

These programs are increasingly competitive. A decade ago, a 700 GMAT was solidly above median at most T15 schools. Today, it sits at or slightly below.

## T25 and Beyond: 660–700

Schools from Tepper and McCombs through Georgetown McDonough and Emory Goizueta typically report medians between 680 and 710. At this tier, a 680+ GMAT keeps you comfortably in range, and a 700+ makes you a strong candidate on the quant side.

For international applicants targeting US programs in this range, keep in mind that GMAT expectations can vary by nationality. Indian and Chinese applicants often face an informal "GMAT tax" — higher score expectations relative to other demographics — because applicant pools from these countries tend to skew higher on standardized tests.

## International Programs

European programs like INSEAD, LBS, and HEC Paris value the GMAT differently than US schools. INSEAD reports a median around 720, while LBS and HEC Paris sit closer to 710. One-year European programs tend to weigh work experience more heavily relative to test scores.

In Asia, CEIBS and NUS hover around 700, while Indian programs (the IIMs, ISB) primarily use the CAT exam domestically. ISB accepts the GMAT with a median around 710 for international applicants.

## The Test-Optional Question

Post-pandemic, several programs went test-optional, and a few have stayed that way. However, submitting a strong GMAT score still helps — it provides a clear data point in your favor. Our data shows that applicants who submit scores above the median have roughly a 15% higher admit rate than comparable test-optional applicants at the same schools.

If you have a strong score, submit it. If you are scoring below the median after significant preparation, the test-optional route may serve you better — provided the rest of your application is compelling.

## What About the GRE?

Most top programs now accept the GRE. Conversion calculators suggest that a 325+ GRE (165V/160Q) roughly maps to a 720+ GMAT, though schools evaluate these independently. The GMAT still carries slightly more weight at finance-focused programs like Wharton and Booth, where quantitative rigor is a core part of the brand.
